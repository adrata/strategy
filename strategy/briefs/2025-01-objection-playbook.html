Battle-Tested Responses to the 12 Most Common Sales Objections

Objections aren't roadblocks—they're signposts. Every objection tells you what the buyer is really worried about. Master these responses and you'll turn resistance into momentum.

Timing & Priority Objections

1. "We're not ready yet"

"This sounds interesting, but we're not ready to make a change right now."

What They Really Mean

I don't see enough urgency to prioritize this over everything else on my plate.

Your Response

"I hear that a lot. Can I ask—what would need to change for this to become a priority? Is it timing, or is there something specific you'd need to see first?"

2. "We need to wait until next quarter/year"

"Let's revisit this in Q2. We have too much going on right now."

What They Really Mean

I'm overwhelmed and this feels like more work, not less.

Your Response

"What if I told you that every week you wait, you're losing roughly [X hours/Y dollars] to the problem you described? Would it be worth 30 minutes to see if we could start fixing that now instead of later?"

3. "Send me some information"

"Just send me an email with more details and I'll take a look."

What They Really Mean

I want to get off this call politely.

Your Response

"Happy to. But honestly, a generic deck won't tell you if this actually solves your problem. What if we do 15 minutes where I can show you exactly how it works for [their specific use case]? If it's not a fit, I'll tell you."

Cost & Value Objections

4. "It's too expensive"

"We don't have the budget for this right now."

What They Really Mean

I don't see how the value exceeds the cost yet.

Your Response

"Help me understand—is it that the price is too high, or that you're not sure it's worth the investment? Because if I can show you a 3x return in the first 90 days, would that change the equation?"

5. "We already have a solution"

"We're using [Competitor] and it's working fine."

What They Really Mean

Switching feels risky and I don't want to admit we made a bad choice.

Your Response

"That's great—you've already validated the need. Out of curiosity, if you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about your current setup, what would it be?"

6. "We can build this ourselves"

"Our engineering team could probably build something similar."

What They Really Mean

I'm not sure if this is a core competency or a distraction.

Your Response

"You definitely could. The question is: should you? What's your engineering team's hourly rate? And what else would they be building if they weren't building this? We've spent 3 years and $10M getting this right. Is that where you want to invest?"

Authority & Decision Objections

7. "I need to talk to my team"

"Let me discuss this internally and get back to you."

What They Really Mean

I'm not the only decision maker, or I'm not sure how to sell this internally.

Your Response

"That makes sense. Who else would need to be involved? Would it help if I joined that conversation to answer any technical or ROI questions directly?"

8. "I'm not the right person"

"You should really be talking to [someone else]."

What They Really Mean

I don't have authority, or I don't want to deal with this.

Your Response

"I appreciate you pointing me in the right direction. Before I reach out to them, would you mind sharing what you think their biggest priority is right now? That way I can make sure I'm not wasting their time."

Trust & Risk Objections

9. "I've never heard of you"

"You're a small company. How do I know you'll be around in 2 years?"

What They Really Mean

I'm worried about the risk of betting on an unproven vendor.

Your Response

"Fair question. We're backed by [investors], we have [X customers] including [notable names], and our retention rate is [Y%]. But the real answer is: would you rather be customer #500 at a big company where you're a number, or customer #50 at a company where we'll bend over backwards to make you successful?"

10. "We tried something like this before"

"We implemented a similar tool last year and it failed."

What They Really Mean

I got burned and I don't want to get burned again.

Your Response

"I'm sorry to hear that. What went wrong? Was it the tool itself, the implementation, or adoption? Because if I can show you specifically how we've solved for those exact failure points, would that be worth 15 minutes?"

11. "This seems too good to be true"

"How do I know this actually works?"

What They Really Mean

I've been oversold before and I'm skeptical of vendor claims.

Your Response

"I'd be skeptical too. Here's what I'd suggest: Let's run a pilot. You pick the use case, we'll set success metrics together, and if we don't hit them, you walk away. No risk. Would that work?"

12. "I need to think about it"

"Let me sleep on it and I'll get back to you."

What They Really Mean

Something is holding me back but I'm not sure what—or I don't want to tell you.

Your Response

"Of course. Can I ask—what specifically do you want to think about? Is it the fit, the timing, or something else? I'd rather address it now than have you wondering."
