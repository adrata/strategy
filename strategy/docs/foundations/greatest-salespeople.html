The Pioneers

John Henry Patterson
Founder, NCR | 1844-1922
In 1884, salesmen were seen as con artists. By 1920, they ran America's biggest companies. One man changed everything.
Patterson bought a failing cash register company. His salesmen were untrained, unscripted, and unpredictable. Sales were random. So he did something radical: he wrote a script. Every word. Every gesture. Every objection handled.

He built a "sales school" where reps practiced until perfect. He invented territories, quotas, and sales conventions. He was fired for being too controlling—twice.

But here's what matters: Thomas Watson Sr. (IBM), Charles Kettering (Delco), and 50+ future CEOs all trained under Patterson. He didn't just build a company. He built a profession.
The Lesson

Selling can be taught. Script it. Practice it. Perfect it.

Thomas Watson Sr.
Founder of IBM | 1874-1956
He was fired at 40. Then he built the most valuable company in the world.
Watson learned everything at NCR under Patterson. They had a falling out. Watson was fired.

At 40, he joined a tiny company called Computing-Tabulating-Recording. He renamed it IBM. And he did one thing differently: his salesmen didn't pitch products. They solved problems.

"THINK" signs hung in every office. Dark suits. White shirts. Perfect posture. IBM salesmen didn't look like salesmen. They looked like executives. Because that's who they were selling to.
The Lesson

Look like your customer. Think like your customer. Be the advisor, not the vendor.

The Record Breakers

Joe Girard
Guinness World Record Holder | 1928-2019
13,001 cars sold 6 per day average
Most car salesmen sell 10 cars a month. Joe Girard sold 6 per day. For 15 years straight. His secret cost $0.50 per customer.
Girard sent 13,000 greeting cards every month. Birthday cards. Holiday cards. "I like you" cards. Just because.

Every customer got a handwritten note. Every buyer got a follow-up call. Every referral got a thank you.

He called it "Girard's Law of 250": Every person knows 250 people who might attend their wedding or funeral. Win one customer, impress them, you have access to 250 more.
The Lesson

Every customer is worth 250 referrals. Treat them that way.

Ben Feldman
New York Life Insurance | 1912-1993
$1.8 billion in policies
He was 5'4" with a lisp. He worked in a town of 20,000 people. He outsold 1,500 of the company's 1,800 offices. Combined.
East Liverpool, Ohio. Population 20,000. No skyscrapers. No Fortune 500 companies. Just Ben Feldman, selling life insurance.

He didn't sell policies. He sold fear. Specific, vivid fear.

"What happens to your family at 8am Monday if you don't come home Sunday night?"

He worked 7 days a week, 52 weeks a year, for 50 years. When asked his secret, he said one word: "Work."
The Lesson

Sell the pain of not buying. Make them feel the consequences of inaction.

The Empire Builders

Madam C.J. Walker
America's First Self-Made Female Millionaire | 1867-1919
First female millionaire 20,000+ sales agents
Born to former slaves. Orphaned at 7. Widowed at 20. Earning $1.50/day as a washerwoman. She became the first self-made female millionaire in American history.
Sarah Breedlove was losing her hair. Stress. Malnutrition. Bad products. She experimented until she found formulas that worked.

Then she sold them. Door-to-door. Demonstrating on herself. Telling her own story.

She didn't just sell hair care. She sold transformation. She sold hope. She sold "if I can do it, you can too."

She built a network of 20,000+ "Walker Agents"—Black women selling to Black women. She made them financially independent.
The Lesson

Your story is your greatest asset. Authenticity sells.

Mary Kay Ash
Founder, Mary Kay Cosmetics | 1918-2001
$3B+ annual revenue Pink Cadillacs
At 45, she was passed over for promotion. The job went to a man she had trained. She quit. Then she built a $3 billion empire to prove them wrong.
Mary Kay spent 25 years in direct sales. She was good. Really good. But the men kept getting promoted over her.

So she retired and wrote a book about her dream company. Then she realized: why not just build it?

With $5,000, she started Mary Kay Cosmetics. Her philosophy: "God first, family second, career third." And: "Praise people to success."

The pink Cadillacs weren't just cars. They were symbols. Proof that women could succeed.
The Lesson

Recognition is more powerful than money. Make your sellers feel celebrated.

Ray Kroc
Founder, McDonald's Corporation | 1902-1984
Started at 52 $190B+ empire today
At 52, Ray Kroc was a milkshake machine salesman. A failing one. Then he visited a hamburger stand that was using 8 of his machines at once. He had to know why.
The McDonald brothers had a system. Fast. Efficient. Consistent. Kroc saw it differently.

He didn't see hamburgers. He saw a business that could be copied. Everywhere.

Kroc didn't sell food. He sold a turnkey business. Every franchisee was a customer buying a complete system.

Today: 40,000+ locations. $190 billion market cap. The milkshake machine salesman built it all because he knew what he was really selling.
The Lesson

Don't sell the product—sell the system. The business model is the product.

The Modern Masters

Zig Ziglar
Motivational Sales Legend | 1926-2012
Millions attended his seminars
He sold pots and pans door-to-door. He was mediocre. Then a manager told him he could be great. That belief changed everything.
Ziglar was struggling. Barely making quota. Ready to quit.

A regional manager saw something in him. Told him: "You could be a great one." Nobody had ever said that before.

Ziglar went from mediocre to national champion. Then he spent 50 years telling others the same thing: You can be great.

"You can have everything in life you want if you will just help enough other people get what they want."
The Lesson

Help enough people get what they want, and you'll get what you want.

Mark Benioff
Founder & CEO, Salesforce | 1964-
$200B+ market cap
He threw parties where guests smashed software CDs with hammers. He hired protesters to picket competitor conferences. He turned B2B software into a movement.
Benioff learned sales at Oracle. Larry Ellison told him: "You have a salesman's personality." He wasn't wrong.

At Salesforce, Benioff didn't sell software. He sold rebellion. "No Software" was the tagline. Software CDs were the enemy. Cloud was the future.

He staged fake protests outside Siebel conferences. He created an enemy and positioned Salesforce as the alternative.

$200 billion market cap says otherwise.
The Lesson

Don't sell products—sell movements. Create an enemy and be the alternative.

What They All Have in Common

1. They were rejected thousands of times

Sanders: 1,009 nos. Blakely: doors slammed daily. They kept going anyway.

2. They showed, not told

Fuller demonstrated brushes. Bissell swept carpets. Popeil peeled vegetables. The product in action sells itself.

3. They made it personal

Walker told her own story. Girard sent 13,000 cards monthly. Relationships drive everything.

4. They sold the transformation

Feldman sold family protection. Mary Kay sold confidence. Outcomes, not features.

5. They gave first

Fuller gave free brushes. Gillette gave free razors. Lauder gave free samples. Reciprocity works.

"Nothing happens until somebody sells something." — Thomas Watson Sr., IBM
