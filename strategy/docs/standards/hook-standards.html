Hook Standards

How to write hooks that stop VPs of Sales at 6 AM. Reference for humans and AI agents.

Step 0: Which archetype(s)? Each archetype has a specific enemy (core fear). The hook must name or evoke that enemy. See Archetypes Persona.

The Hook Test

Would a VP of Sales at 23% pipeline coverage, checking their phone at 6 AM, stop scrolling to read this? If no, rewrite.

The 5 Hook Patterns

1. The Arrival

Something happens. Time, place, action. Immediate scene. No preamble.

"The email arrives at 6:47 AM. I'm already awake."

Best for: Story-based content, narrative articles, Q4-style writing

2. The Number

A specific, surprising, or emotionally weighted number.

"$4.2 million against $18.4 million. The coverage ratio isn't just bad—it's mathematically impossible."

Best for: Strategy docs, data-driven content, anyone who respects hard numbers

3. The Confession

Vulnerability that creates trust and curiosity. Admission of failure or fear.

"I lost a $150K deal last month. Here's the mistake I'll never make again."

Best for: Rebuilding Leader, Recovering Performer, Hungry Underdog archetypes

4. The Reversal

Conventional wisdom is wrong. Set up the expectation, then break it.

"Everyone says discovery is about asking great questions. Everyone is wrong."

Best for: Frustrated Fighter, Anxious Elite—people who distrust conventional wisdom

5. The Question

A question they can't answer but desperately want to.

"Who besides your champion has to approve this purchase?"

Best for: Any archetype—the question should match their specific fear

Hook Failures

Avoid:

  • × Generic opening ("In today's fast-paced world...")
  • × Question with obvious answer ("Want more pipeline?")
  • × Promising without specificity ("Game-changing insights")
  • × Sounding like marketing copy
  • × Starting with "We" or your company name
