Core Principle

We write for VPs of Sales in survival mode. They don't want education—they want validation, ammunition, and quick wins. Every piece of content must feel like GAINING insight, not being sold to.

1. Voice & Tone

Our voice is modeled on Q4: Chapter 1. Study it before creating any narrative content.

Voice Traits

Visceral
Emotions land physically. "The thought lands in my chest like a stone." Not: "I felt worried."
Specific
Exact numbers, names, times. "$18.4 million. 88 days. 6:47 AM." Not: "A large target over the next few months."
First-Person
The reader IS the VP of Sales. They see themselves in every scene. "I swing my legs out of bed."
Show, Don't Tell
Reveal psychology through actions and scenes. Marco stops making eye contact = tension, without saying "things were tense."
Questions that Pull
End sections with questions that create curiosity. "And what happens when I finally ask?"
Varied Rhythm
Short punchy sentences. Then longer ones that build tension. "The numbers haven't changed overnight. They never do."

Tone Guidelines

Do

  • Write like a trusted peer, not a vendor
  • Acknowledge the pressure they're under
  • Use "we" when discussing shared challenges
  • Let tension sit—don't rush to resolve it
  • Make them feel seen, not sold

Don't

  • Sound like marketing copy
  • Use buzzwords (synergy, leverage, empower)
  • Oversimplify their problems
  • Promise outcomes we can't deliver
  • Lecture or explain things they already know
Example: Q4 Opening
"The email arrives at 6:47 AM. I'm already awake. I've been awake since 4:30, running the numbers in my head for the hundredth time. $18.4 million. That's the Q4 target the board approved. $18.4 million in new bookings across 88 days."
Immediate immersion. Specific time. The anxiety is shown, not stated. The math is precise. We're in their head from sentence one.

2. Hooks

The first sentence determines whether they read the rest. Hooks create an open loop that demands resolution.

Hook Patterns That Work

The Arrival

Something happens. Time, place, action. Immediate scene.

"The email arrives at 6:47 AM."

The Number

A specific, surprising, or weighted number.

"$4.2 million against $18.4 million. The coverage ratio isn't just bad—it's mathematically impossible."

The Confession

Vulnerability that creates trust and curiosity.

"I lost a $150K deal last month. Here's the mistake I'll never make again."

The Reversal

Conventional wisdom is wrong. Set up the expectation, then break it.

"Everyone says discovery is about asking great questions. Everyone is wrong."

The Question

A question they can't answer but desperately want to.

"Who besides your champion has to approve this purchase?"
Hook Test
Would a VP of Sales stop scrolling to read this? If they're at 23% pipeline coverage and checking their phone at 6 AM, does this hook grab them?

3. Stories

Stories are the delivery vehicle for insight. A fact is forgettable. A story that reveals the fact is unforgettable.

Story Structure

  1. Scene: Time, place, sensory detail. Ground them in a moment.
  2. Tension: Something is at stake. A gap between what they want and what they have.
  3. Turn: A moment of realization. Something shifts.
  4. Insight: What they now understand that they didn't before.
Example: The Sofia Question

"Sofia asks the question that breaks something open in my mind.

'On the Garrison deal—the $2.1 million one—who besides the CTO has to approve this purchase?'

I pause. 'Adrian is the decision maker.'

'The CTO makes $2.1 million purchase decisions alone at a 4,000-person financial services company?'

I open my mouth. Close it."

Scene (boardroom), Tension (the math doesn't work), Turn (Sofia's question), Insight (Adrian isn't the decision maker). The reader realizes it at the same moment as the protagonist.

Story Principles

  • Specificity creates credibility. "Adrian Marchetti, CTO" not "a senior leader."
  • Dialogue reveals character. "Don't worry about all that. I'll handle it internally." One line tells you everything about the deal's real status.
  • Let readers draw conclusions. Don't explain the insight—show the scene and let them feel it.
  • Earned emotion. If you haven't built the scene, the emotion won't land.

4. Offers

Offers are how we earn the right to a conversation. The goal is PULL—they rip the offer out of our hands because they need it.

The Offer Test

Can they do this themselves? If yes, the offer won't create pull. If no—if only we can deliver it at this speed and quality—they have a reason to engage.

Offer Principles

1. Align with Emotional Drivers

Every archetype has a core fear (enemy). The offer must address that specific fear, not a generic pain point.

2. Don't Demand Unearned Trust

A stranger asking to "audit your pipeline" requires trust they haven't built. An offer to "map the buying committee for 5 of your accounts in 48 hours" requires only access to public information and account names.

Earned Trust

  • "Give us 5 accounts, we'll map the buying committees"
  • "We'll do the work—you keep the output regardless"
  • "No CRM access needed. Just account names."

Unearned Trust

  • "Let us audit your pipeline"
  • "Connect your CRM for a health check"
  • "Share your deal data for analysis"

3. Don't Offer What They Can Do Internally

"We'll review your tech stack" → "I'll just have my ops person do that."

"We'll map buying committees with 90% accuracy in 48 hours" → They literally cannot do this.

4. Differentiation is Everything

The offer must leverage something only Adrata can do. Our differentiator: instantly map any buying committee with pain intelligence and personal archetypes for each stakeholder.

Offer Validation
Would a VP of Sales forward this to their CEO as proof they're doing something about the problem? Would they tell a peer about it? If the offer creates something they'd share or use immediately, it works.

5. Archetypes (ICA)

Ideal Customer Archetypes (ICA) are psychological profiles, not demographics. Each has a name, an enemy (core fear), and a specific emotional driver.

The Core Archetypes

The Anxious Elite
Enemy: Pipeline Exposure — What they don't see will destroy them
Crushed it before. Now pipeline is drying up and they can't figure out why. Sophisticated, skeptical, suspects something is wrong but can't pinpoint it. Needs validation of gut instinct, not education.
Converts because: We validate their suspicion. They already know something's off—we prove it with data they can't get elsewhere.
The Worried Darling
Enemy: Golden Trap — Success that's actually fragile, one quarter from collapse
Hitting numbers but knows it's luck/timing, not system. Can't admit vulnerability publicly. Needs private, confidential solutions.
Converts because: We become their secret weapon. Private intelligence that protects without exposing.
The Stressed Performer
Enemy: Silent Slip — Deals quietly dying while everyone focuses on new pipeline
Working hard but feels like running in sand. Needs ammunition to justify resources, headcount, attention from leadership.
Converts because: We give them data they can take to their CEO. Proof that the problem is real and solvable.
The Rebuilding Leader
Enemy: Miss Pattern — Inherited a broken machine, needs to prove previous regime was wrong
Has mandate and resources. Wants to prove they're different. Looking for partners, not vendors.
Converts because: Motivated + resourced = ideal buyer. They WANT to spend to prove their approach works.
The Hungry Comeback
Enemy: Resource Gap — Competing against teams 10x their size with 1/10th the budget
Scrappy, ambitious, chip on shoulder. Wants unfair advantages. Willing to try unconventional approaches.
Converts because: We offer the unfair advantage. "The thing your bigger competitors don't know."

Using Archetypes

  • Every piece of content should target 1-2 archetypes. Generic content converts nobody.
  • Lead with their enemy. Name the fear before offering the solution.
  • Match offer to archetype. Don't offer "break into new accounts" to someone with a full pipeline—offer "protect what you have."
  • Speak their language. The Anxious Elite doesn't want education. The Rebuilding Leader wants partnership.

6. Strategy Documents

Strategy documents propose new approaches. They must answer fundamental questions upfront—don't make readers dig for the decision-relevant information.

Required Sections

Open with: What is the specific recommendation? State it in one sentence before explaining.

Fundamental Questions (Answer These Upfront)

  1. What are we recommending? — Clear, specific recommendation in one sentence.
  2. Why now? — What changed that makes this the right move today?
  3. What's the alternative? — What happens if we don't do this? What's the status quo cost?
  4. What's the evidence? — Data, benchmarks, research that supports this recommendation.
  5. What are the risks? — Be honest. What could go wrong? How do we mitigate?
  6. What does success look like? — Specific metrics. How will we know it's working?
  7. What's the ask? — Resources, budget, time, approval needed.
Example: LinkedIn Outbound Strategy

Recommendation: While ads ramp up, prioritize organic content + warm DMs over cold calling. Warm outreach converts 10x higher than cold.

Why now: Ads take 5 weeks to generate pipeline. We have capacity now. Cold calling success rates have dropped to 2.3% (down from 4.8% last year).

Alternative: Continue cold calling at 2.3% success rate, burning rep time with diminishing returns.

Evidence: LinkedIn research: 61% higher conversion when prospects see organic + paid content vs. paid alone. Warm DM response: 15-25% vs. cold: 1-3%.

Format Standards

  • Executive summary first. The busy reader should get the core message in 30 seconds.
  • Progressive disclosure. Summary → Evidence → Details → Appendix.
  • Scannable. Use headers, bullets, bold for key points. No walls of text.
  • Specific numbers. "$18.4M" not "significant revenue." "61% higher" not "much better."
  • Link to supporting docs. Don't duplicate—reference the full playbook, the archetype doc, etc.

7. Pre-Publish Checklist

Before Publishing Any Content

  • Does the hook stop a stressed VP at 6 AM?
  • Is this for 1-2 specific archetypes, not everyone?
  • Does it name their enemy (core fear)?
  • Is there something they literally can't do themselves?
  • Does it feel like gaining insight, not being sold to?
  • Are all numbers specific, not vague?
  • Have I shown, not told?
  • Would they forward this to a peer?
  • Does the offer require only earned trust?
  • Is the CTA clear and low-friction?

Strategy Document Checklist

  • Is the recommendation stated in one sentence at the top?
  • Have I answered "why now"?
  • Have I stated the alternative (status quo cost)?
  • Is evidence specific and sourced?
  • Have I acknowledged risks honestly?
  • Is success measurable with specific metrics?
  • Is the ask clear?
The Ultimate Test

If a VP of Sales at 23% pipeline coverage reads this at 6 AM, do they feel more capable, more informed, and more confident in their next move? If yes, publish it. If no, rewrite it.
