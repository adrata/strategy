The Greatest Salespeople in History

The stories. The hooks. The lessons.

"Everyone lives by selling something." — Robert Louis Stevenson

The Pioneers

John Henry Patterson
Founder, NCR | 1844-1922
In 1884, salesmen were seen as con artists. By 1920, they ran America's biggest companies. One man changed everything.
Patterson bought a failing cash register company. His salesmen were untrained, unscripted, and unpredictable. Sales were random. So he did something radical: he wrote a script. Every word. Every gesture. Every objection handled.

He built a "sales school" where reps practiced until perfect. He invented territories, quotas, and sales conventions. He was fired for being too controlling—twice.

But here's what matters: Thomas Watson Sr. (IBM), Charles Kettering (Delco), and 50+ future CEOs all trained under Patterson. He didn't just build a company. He built a profession.
The Lesson

Selling can be taught. Script it. Practice it. Perfect it.

Thomas Watson Sr.
Founder of IBM | 1874-1956
He was fired at 40. Then he built the most valuable company in the world.
Watson learned everything at NCR under Patterson. They had a falling out. Watson was fired.

At 40, he joined a tiny company called Computing-Tabulating-Recording. He renamed it IBM. And he did one thing differently: his salesmen didn't pitch products. They solved problems.

"THINK" signs hung in every office. Dark suits. White shirts. Perfect posture. IBM salesmen didn't look like salesmen. They looked like executives. Because that's who they were selling to.

The result? IBM dominated computing for 50 years. Not because of better technology. Because of better selling.
The Lesson

Look like your customer. Think like your customer. Be the advisor, not the vendor.

Elmer Wheeler
"America's Greatest Salesman" | 1903-1968
He tested 105,000 phrases on 19 million customers. Then he found the 5 words that changed everything.
Wheeler was obsessed with one question: What words actually work?

He tested everything. Headlines. Openers. Closes. Objection handlers. On 19 million real customers. The data revealed patterns nobody expected.

His most famous finding: "Don't sell the steak—sell the sizzle."

Emotion beats logic. Every time. Woolworth's implemented his phrases and saw 300% sales increase. His book sold millions. All from testing words.
The Lesson

Sell the sizzle, not the steak. Test your words. The right phrase can 10x results.

The Record Breakers

Joe Girard
Guinness World Record Holder | 1928-2019
13,001 cars sold 6 per day average
Most car salesmen sell 10 cars a month. Joe Girard sold 6 per day. For 15 years straight. His secret cost $0.50 per customer.
Girard sent 13,000 greeting cards every month. Birthday cards. Holiday cards. "I like you" cards. Just because.

Every customer got a handwritten note. Every buyer got a follow-up call. Every referral got a thank you.

He called it "Girard's Law of 250": Every person knows 250 people who might attend their wedding or funeral. Win one customer, impress them, you have access to 250 more. Disappoint one, 250 hear about it.

13,001 cars. One relationship at a time.
The Lesson

Every customer is worth 250 referrals. Treat them that way.

Ben Feldman
New York Life Insurance | 1912-1993
$1.8 billion in policies
He was 5'4" with a lisp. He worked in a town of 20,000 people. He outsold 1,500 of the company's 1,800 offices. Combined.
East Liverpool, Ohio. Population 20,000. No skyscrapers. No Fortune 500 companies. Just Ben Feldman, selling life insurance.

He didn't sell policies. He sold fear. Specific, vivid fear.

"What happens to your family at 8am Monday if you don't come home Sunday night?"

He worked 7 days a week, 52 weeks a year, for 50 years. When asked his secret, he said one word: "Work."

In his peak years, he sold more than entire agencies in New York and Chicago. From a town smaller than most suburbs.
The Lesson

Sell the pain of not buying. Make them feel the consequences of inaction.

Erica Feidner
"The Piano Matchmaker" | 1990s-2000s
$150,000+ average sale
She sold $150,000 pianos to the world's pickiest customers. Her sales pitch? She didn't have one.
Concert pianists will play the same instrument for 40 years. They don't buy a piano. They marry one.

Feidner understood this. She never pitched. Never pushed. Never mentioned price until asked. She let musicians play every piano in the showroom. For hours. For days. For weeks.

She called herself a "matchmaker." Her job wasn't selling. It was listening.

Billy Joel. Lang Lang. Symphony orchestras. They all bought from Erica. Because she found them the one.
The Lesson

For major purchases, be a matchmaker. Help them find what they already want.

The Door-to-Door Legends

Alfred Fuller
Founder, Fuller Brush Company | 1885-1973
25,000 salespeople at peak
He couldn't hold a job. He failed at everything. So at 21, he started making brushes in his basement. Then he knocked on every door in Boston.
Fuller had a problem: nobody wanted to buy brushes from a stranger.

So he gave them away. A free brush at every door. Whether they bought or not.

The gift created obligation. The demonstration created desire. The follow-up created sales.

"The Fuller Brush Man" became an American icon. 25,000 salespeople. $130 million in revenue. All from giving before asking.
The Lesson

Give before you ask. A free sample opens every door.

Anna Bissell
First Female CEO of a Major Corporation | 1846-1934
Her husband invented the carpet sweeper. Then he died. At 43, she became America's first female CEO. Her competitors said she'd fail within a year.
1889. Women didn't run companies. They weren't supposed to.

Anna Bissell didn't care. She'd been selling sweepers door-to-door alongside her husband for years. She knew the business better than anyone.

Her secret: never explain what the product does. Show it. She'd walk into a home, sweep the carpet in front of them, and let the results speak.

She expanded to Europe. She opened factories. The brand still exists 130 years later. All because she understood: demonstration beats explanation.
The Lesson

Show, don't tell. A demonstration is worth a thousand words.

David H. McConnell
Founder, Avon | 1858-1937
6 million salespeople today
He was a door-to-door book salesman who couldn't sell books. So he gave away free perfume samples. The samples outsold the books. He never sold another book again.
McConnell noticed something strange: housewives didn't want his books. They wanted the free perfume he gave with each purchase.

So he switched. But he had another insight: women trusted other women. Especially in 1886, when inviting a male stranger into your home was unusual.

He hired women. Only women. Called them "Avon Ladies."

Today: 6 million Avon representatives worldwide. One of the largest sales forces in history. All because he listened to what customers actually wanted.
The Lesson

Hire people who look like your customers. Trust is easier when the seller feels familiar.

The Empire Builders

Madam C.J. Walker
America's First Self-Made Female Millionaire | 1867-1919
First female millionaire 20,000+ sales agents
Born to former slaves. Orphaned at 7. Widowed at 20. Earning $1.50/day as a washerwoman. She became the first self-made female millionaire in American history. Here's how.
Sarah Breedlove was losing her hair. Stress. Malnutrition. Bad products. She experimented until she found formulas that worked.

Then she sold them. Door-to-door. Demonstrating on herself. Telling her own story.

She didn't just sell hair care. She sold transformation. She sold hope. She sold "if I can do it, you can too."

She built a network of 20,000+ "Walker Agents"—Black women selling to Black women. She made them financially independent. She changed their lives.

When she died at 51, she was worth over $1 million. Today that's $15 million. All from a $1.50/day washerwoman.
The Lesson

Your story is your greatest asset. Authenticity sells.

Mary Kay Ash
Founder, Mary Kay Cosmetics | 1918-2001
$3B+ annual revenue Pink Cadillacs
At 45, she was passed over for promotion. The job went to a man she had trained. She quit. Then she built a $3 billion empire to prove them wrong.
Mary Kay spent 25 years in direct sales. She was good. Really good. But the men kept getting promoted over her.

So she retired and wrote a book about her dream company. Then she realized: why not just build it?

With $5,000, she started Mary Kay Cosmetics. Her philosophy: "God first, family second, career third." And: "Praise people to success."

The pink Cadillacs weren't just cars. They were symbols. Proof that women could succeed. Proof that recognition matters more than money.

3.5 million consultants. Billions in revenue. The woman they wouldn't promote built something bigger than all of them.
The Lesson

Recognition is more powerful than money. Make your sellers feel celebrated.

Estée Lauder
Founder, Estée Lauder Companies | 1906-2004
$15B+ company today
She had no stores. No advertising budget. No investors. She had one thing: she would personally put cream on a stranger's face at a department store counter. Nobody could say no.
Lauder understood something profound: skin care is intimate. You can't describe how a cream feels. You have to feel it.

So she touched people. Literally. She'd approach women, apply product herself, massage it in. An intimate moment with a stranger.

She invented "gift with purchase." Buy one thing, get another free. It's now standard. She invented it.

At 80, she still worked the counters. Still touching faces. Still closing sales.

"Telephone, telegraph, tell a woman," she said. She understood word-of-mouth before it had a name.
The Lesson

Get the product into their hands. Free samples create customers.

Ray Kroc
Founder, McDonald's Corporation | 1902-1984
Started at 52 $190B+ empire today
At 52, Ray Kroc was a milkshake machine salesman. A failing one. Then he visited a hamburger stand that was using 8 of his machines at once. He had to know why.
The McDonald brothers had a system. Fast. Efficient. Consistent. Kroc saw it differently.

He didn't see hamburgers. He saw a business that could be copied. Everywhere.

He convinced the brothers to let him franchise it. Then he bought them out. For $2.7 million. They thought they'd won.

Kroc didn't sell food. He sold a turnkey business. Every franchisee was a customer buying a complete system.

Today: 40,000+ locations. $190 billion market cap. The milkshake machine salesman built it all because he knew what he was really selling.
The Lesson

Don't sell the product—sell the system. The business model is the product.

Colonel Harland Sanders
Founder, Kentucky Fried Chicken | 1890-1980
Started at 65 1,009 rejections
He was 65 years old. Broke. His restaurant had just failed. His first Social Security check was $105. He used it to start selling fried chicken recipes out of his car. He was rejected 1,009 times.
A new highway bypassed Sanders' restaurant. Business died. At 65, he was done.

But he had one thing: a fried chicken recipe people loved. So he got in his car and drove to restaurants.

His pitch: "Let me cook chicken in your kitchen. If you like it, pay me 5 cents per chicken sold."

He slept in his car. Ate in diners. Got rejected. Over. And over. And over.

1,009 restaurants said no. Number 1,010 said yes.

By 1964, he had 600 franchises. He sold the company for $2 million and became the face of KFC until his death.
The Lesson

1,009 rejections mean nothing if #1,010 says yes. Keep. Going.

The Entertainers

Ron Popeil
"The King of Infomercials" | 1935-2021
$2B+ in sales
"But wait, there's more!" Those four words made Ron Popeil a billionaire. He didn't invent the infomercial. He perfected it.
Popeil learned sales on Chicago's Maxwell Street, pitching to crowds. Every infomercial was that same pitch, broadcast to millions.

His formula was simple:
1. Show the problem
2. Demonstrate the solution
3. Stack the bonuses
4. Create urgency
5. Make it easy to buy

Veg-O-Matic. Pocket Fisherman. Showtime Rotisserie. Over $2 billion in sales. From a guy who started on a street corner.
The Lesson

Selling is entertainment. Make it a show.

Joe Ades
"The Gentleman Peeler" | 1934-2009
He sold $5 vegetable peelers on the streets of Manhattan. He wore $2,000 Savile Row suits. He lived in a luxury apartment. He dined at the finest restaurants. From $5 peelers.
Joe Ades didn't sell peelers. He sold a performance.

"It'll julienne, it'll shred, it'll make a rose out of a tomato. Watch this!"

His British accent. His tailored suits. His theatrical flourishes. People didn't buy because they needed peelers. They bought because they wanted to have met Joe Ades.

He made millions. From $5 peelers. In Union Square.

His funeral drew hundreds. For a vegetable peeler salesman.
The Lesson

People buy the experience, not the product. Charisma makes anything valuable.

Billy Mays
TV Pitchman | 1958-2009
OxiClean, Mighty Putty, Kaboom
He was the loudest man on television. People mocked him. Comedians parodied him. He didn't care. He was too busy selling $1 billion in products.
"HI, BILLY MAYS HERE!"

That voice. That beard. That energy. You couldn't ignore him. That was the point.

Mays started on the Atlantic City Boardwalk. Same streets as Joe Ades. He learned that in a world of boring, energy wins.

His volume wasn't a gimmick. It was strategy. Attention is the first sale. If they don't notice you, they can't buy from you.

OxiClean alone did $1 billion. All because he refused to be ignored.
The Lesson

Energy is contagious. Stand out by being impossible to ignore.

The Strategists

King Camp Gillette
Founder, Gillette | 1855-1932
He gave away 3.5 million razors for free. It was the most profitable decision in business history.
Gillette had a problem: his razors were too good. People only needed to buy one.

So he flipped the model. Sell razors cheap—or give them away. Make money on the blades.

During World War I, he gave a free razor to every American soldier. 3.5 million razors. 36 million blades.

When those soldiers came home, they were Gillette customers for life. Every one of them needed blades.

Today we call it "razor and blades." Printers/ink. Consoles/games. Keurig/pods. Gillette invented it.
The Lesson

Give away the razor, sell the blades. Optimize for lifetime value.

Dale Carnegie
Author, "How to Win Friends and Influence People" | 1888-1955
30 million copies sold
He failed as an actor. Failed as a salesman. Failed as a novelist. At 24, he was teaching public speaking at a YMCA for $2 per class. That class became a 30-million-copy book.
Carnegie's principles seem obvious now. That's because he made them obvious.

Be genuinely interested in others. Smile. Remember names. Listen more than you talk. Make people feel important.

Before Carnegie, nobody had written this down. After Carnegie, every sales training program borrowed from him.

Warren Buffett credits Carnegie's course with transforming his life. He still has the certificate on his wall.

30 million copies. 89 years in print. The failed actor taught the world to sell.
The Lesson

Be genuinely interested in other people. Influence follows interest.

David Ogilvy
"Father of Advertising" | 1911-1999
Before he was the "Father of Advertising," he sold cooking stoves door-to-door in Scotland. The manual he wrote for that job was called "the finest sales instruction ever written" by Fortune magazine.
Ogilvy sold AGA cooking stoves. Big, expensive, complicated stoves. To skeptical Scottish housewives.

His rules: Don't sit until invited. Don't smoke unless offered. Never wear a hat. Study your customer's business.

And his most important rule: "The more you tell, the more you sell."

He brought these principles to advertising. Rolls-Royce: "At 60 miles an hour, the loudest noise comes from the electric clock." Long copy. Detailed facts. Respect for the reader's intelligence.

Ogilvy & Mather became one of the world's great agencies. All built on lessons from door-to-door sales.
The Lesson

The more you tell, the more you sell. Never underestimate the customer's intelligence.

The Modern Masters

Zig Ziglar
Motivational Sales Legend | 1926-2012
Millions attended his seminars
He sold pots and pans door-to-door. He was mediocre. Then a manager told him he could be great. That belief changed everything.
Ziglar was struggling. Barely making quota. Ready to quit.

A regional manager saw something in him. Told him: "You could be a great one." Nobody had ever said that before.

Ziglar went from mediocre to national champion. Then he spent 50 years telling others the same thing: You can be great.

"You can have everything in life you want if you will just help enough other people get what they want."

That belief—that sales is about helping—made him the most beloved sales trainer in history.
The Lesson

Help enough people get what they want, and you'll get what you want.

Mark Benioff
Founder & CEO, Salesforce | 1964-
$200B+ market cap
He threw parties where guests smashed software CDs with hammers. He hired protesters to picket competitor conferences. He turned B2B software into a movement.
Benioff learned sales at Oracle. Larry Ellison told him: "You have a salesman's personality." He wasn't wrong.

At Salesforce, Benioff didn't sell software. He sold rebellion. "No Software" was the tagline. Software CDs were the enemy. Cloud was the future.

He staged fake protests outside Siebel conferences. He created an enemy and positioned Salesforce as the alternative.

Enterprise software became tribal. Emotional. Us vs. them. It wasn't supposed to work.

$200 billion market cap says otherwise.
The Lesson

Don't sell products—sell movements. Create an enemy and be the alternative.

The Unlikely Sellers

Sara Blakely
Founder, Spanx | 1971-
Sold fax machines for 7 years Youngest self-made female billionaire
She sold fax machines door-to-door for 7 years. Doors slammed in her face daily. Gates closed on her car. She was escorted out by security. It was the best training she ever got.
Blakely's fax machine territory was Florida. Hot. Humid. Nobody wanted to buy.

She learned to love rejection. To expect it. To push through it. Every "no" was practice for the next "yes."

When she invented Spanx, she cold-called Neiman Marcus. Got laughed at. Got a meeting anyway. Demonstrated the product in the bathroom—on herself.

She kept her fax machine job for two years after launching. Did her own PR. Hand-delivered products.

Youngest self-made female billionaire. All because door-to-door taught her that rejection is just practice.
The Lesson

Rejection is training. Get rejected early and often. It builds tolerance.

Mark Cuban
Billionaire Investor | 1958-
Sold garbage bags at age 12
At 12 years old, he wanted basketball shoes. His dad said earn them. So he went door-to-door selling garbage bags. He learned more in that summer than in four years of business school.
No fancy product. No technology. Just garbage bags and a kid who wanted basketball shoes.

Cuban learned the basics: find customers, make a pitch, handle rejection, close. He learned that nobody buys on the first visit. He learned to follow up.

Later, he got fired from a computer store for closing a deal instead of opening the store. He started his own company. Sold it for $6 million.

Then Broadcast.com. Sold to Yahoo for $5.7 billion.

"Sales cures all," he says. "If you're a great salesperson, you can always find a job."
The Lesson

Sales cures all. Learn to sell and you'll never go hungry.

Barbara Corcoran
Founder, The Corcoran Group | 1949-
$1,000 loan to $6B empire
She failed at 20 jobs before age 23. She couldn't spell. She couldn't write. Her boyfriend gave her $1,000 and dumped her for her secretary. She turned that $1,000 into a $6 billion real estate empire. Partly out of spite.
When her boyfriend left, he told her she'd never succeed without him.

Corcoran didn't get sad. She got competitive. She decided to build something bigger than anything he could imagine.

Her genius was PR. She created "The Corcoran Report"—a marketing piece disguised as market research. Newspapers quoted it. She staged newsworthy events. She manufactured attention.

"The difference between successful people and everyone else is that successful people just keep getting up."

She sold The Corcoran Group for $66 million. Her ex watched.
The Lesson

Attention is the first sale. Create news, don't wait for it.

What They All Have in Common

1. They were rejected thousands of times

Sanders: 1,009 nos. Blakely: doors slammed daily. Corcoran: failed at 20 jobs. They kept going anyway.

2. They showed, not told

Fuller demonstrated brushes. Bissell swept carpets. Popeil peeled vegetables. The product in action sells itself.

3. They made it personal

Walker told her own story. Girard sent 13,000 cards monthly. Feidner spent hours listening. Relationships drive everything.

4. They sold the transformation

Feldman sold family protection. Mary Kay sold confidence. Blakely sold how you feel in your clothes. Outcomes, not features.

5. They gave first

Fuller gave free brushes. Gillette gave free razors. Lauder gave free samples. Reciprocity works.

6. They stood out

Billy Mays was loud. Joe Ades was elegant. Benioff was rebellious. In a world of boring, different wins.

7. They systemized success

Patterson created training. Watson built processes. Kroc franchised a system. The best salespeople are also the best teachers.

8. They saw sales as the ultimate skill

Every billionaire here—Cuban, Blakely, Kroc, Benioff—credits sales as their foundation. It unlocks everything else.

"Nothing happens until somebody sells something." — Thomas Watson Sr., IBM
