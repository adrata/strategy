Game UX Inspired (Scott Jenson)

"Fun is just another word for learning." Games teach without users knowing they're learning. Apply this to sharing. — Raph Koster, A Theory of Fun
Story vs Narrative: Design the sharing journey
Users construct their own narrative. Design tight story arcs so the narrative they create is "I have to share this." Don't leave gaps they fill with confusion.
The Mac boot sequence approach
Mac 1984 started with a promise (smiling Mac) that "inflated" into reality. Your onboarding should start with a promise of the value they'll share later.
Fractal sharing loops
Break "sharing" into nested layers: 1) Experience value 2) Feel compelled 3) Know how to share 4) Actually share 5) Friend signs up. Each layer needs its own feedback.
One action, multiple sharing variations
Like Mario's jump (basic, long, landing), make "share" feel like one action with natural variations: share a win, share a report, invite a colleague. Same muscle, different contexts.
The Frogger principle
Start in safe zone (using product alone), then street (sharing internally), then river (sharing externally), then parking (converting friends). Progressive challenge.
Multi-variant affordances
When something is shareable, show multiple subtle signals: visual highlight, slight animation, share icon appears. Encode shareability in multiple ways like games encode stamina.
Grace notes for sharing
Small delightful micro-interactions that prep users for sharing. Like flushing toilets in Half-Life — meaningless but trains you to try things. Small "share" hints everywhere.
The stamina wheel for virality
Breath of the Wild shows stamina + rate of drain. Show users their "sharing potential" — how many people they've reached, how close to next reward. Make progress visible.
Hintiness, not popups
Games hint subtly and repeatedly until you get it. Don't popup "INVITE FRIENDS!" Instead, subtly show others sharing, show the reward, animate from ruler like Photoshop guide.
Prevent sharing "bottom feeders"
Games call it "bottom feeding" when players stay at easy levels forever. Users who use but never share are bottom feeders. Use hintiness to move them to sharing loops.
Moon chains for referrals
In Mario Odyssey, you can always see the next moon. After every share action, show the NEXT sharing opportunity. Always visible, never pushy.
The broken wall pattern
In Zelda, smooth walls are normal but broken patterns signal "something interesting here." Make shareable content visually distinct — a subtle pattern that says "share me."
Start with ONE sharing action
WoW starts with 1 attack spell, not 50. Don't show referral program + share buttons + invite team on day 1. Start with ONE: "Share this report." Build from there.
Pacing the viral journey
Day 1: Use product. Day 3: See first shareable output. Day 7: Share internally. Day 14: Share externally. Day 30: Formal referral. Progressive unlocking.
Mixed visual styles for shareability
Games use different styles for "me vs world." Use distinct visual style for "my work" vs "shareable outputs" — makes exports feel special and share-worthy.
The grasshopper principle
In Zelda, grasshoppers fly from grass — atmospheric but teaches you to cut grass later. Add small ambient hints that prep users for sharing actions they'll take later.
Animate the origin of shares
When user creates a report, animate it "emerging" from the data. Like Photoshop guide suggestion: show where shares come from so users discover they can do it directly.
Juicy sharing feedback
Games are "juicy" — lots of feedback per action. When someone shares, make it FEEL good: sound, animation, confetti, counter incrementing. Reward the behavior richly.
The second derivative
Breath of the Wild shows stamina AND rate of use (sweat, leaning). Show not just "you've shared 5 times" but "you're sharing more than last week" — the trend, not just the count.
Teach sharing without teaching
The game taught Jenson to take less steep routes without him knowing. Design the product so users learn to share without realizing they're being taught. Pure behavioral design.

Aha Moment Design

Define your "aha moment"
Facebook: 7 friends in 10 days. Slack: 2000 messages. What's Adrata's? "Saw buyer group for first time"? "Shortened first sales cycle"? Find it and optimize for it.
Time to aha under 5 minutes
Test Drive should deliver aha in under 5 minutes. Enter company → see buyer group → mind blown. No signup friction before the aha.
Aha → share in same session
Immediately after aha moment, surface share option. "Wow, this is useful! Share with colleague?" Strike while iron is hot.
Screenshot-worthy aha
Design the aha moment screen to be screenshot-worthy. Clean, impressive, shareable. Users naturally screenshot things that blow their mind.
Aha moment for the recipient too
When someone receives a shared report, THEY should have an aha moment too. The share itself should be impressive, not just a link to signup.
Stack aha moments
First aha: buyer group. Second aha: PULL score. Third aha: AI insights. Each aha is a potential share moment. Design a sequence.
Aha for different personas
SDR aha: "I know who to call." Manager aha: "I see my team's pipeline." VP aha: "I see why deals stall." Different ahas for different sharers.

Friction Removal

One-click share with pre-written message
Don't make users write the share message. Pre-write it perfectly. "Check out this buyer group for [Company] — found it in 30 seconds."
Share without login for recipient
Shared reports should be viewable without signup. Signup prompt comes AFTER they see value. Remove friction to experiencing the aha.
Mobile-first sharing
Most shares happen on mobile (LinkedIn, iMessage, Slack). Ensure shared links look beautiful on mobile. Test your Open Graph images on phones.
Copy link with one tap
Obvious but often missing. Big "Copy Link" button that shows "Copied!" feedback. Reduce taps to share.
Share to specific apps
Don't just "Share" — offer "Share to Slack", "Share to LinkedIn", "Email to team". Pre-formatted for each platform.
Remember who they've shared with
"Share with Dan again?" Show recent recipients. Make re-sharing to same people effortless.
Bulk invite from contacts
Import from Google Contacts / LinkedIn. Select multiple. Invite all at once. Don't make them type emails one by one.
QR code for in-person sharing
At conferences, meetings, lunches. "Scan this to see the buyer group I was telling you about." Physical to digital bridge.
